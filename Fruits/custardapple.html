Custard Apple - The Sweet Delight

A creamy, luscious fruit with ancient roots and royal connections

About Custard Apple

Custard Apple (Annona squamosa), also known as Sugar Apple or Sitaphal, is a tropical fruit native to the West Indies and South America but has been cultivated in India for centuries. It's characterized by its heart-shaped appearance, knobby green exterior, and sweet, creamy white flesh with black seeds.


Known as "Sitaphal" in Hindi, "Seethaphal" in Telugu, "Sitappazham" in Tamil, and "Aatha" in Malayalam, this fruit gets its Sanskrit name from the goddess Sita, wife of Lord Rama in the Hindu epic Ramayana. Legend has it that Sita favored this fruit during her exile, hence the name "Sita's fruit" or Sitaphal.

Varieties of Custard Apple in India

  • Balanagar - Popular in Telangana and Andhra Pradesh, known for sweetness
  • Red Sitaphal - A rare variety with reddish-purple flesh
  • Island Gem - Known for its large size and reduced number of seeds
  • Mammoth - As the name suggests, produces larger fruits
  • Arka Sahan - Developed by IIHR, featuring better pulp recovery
  • Washington - An introduced variety with excellent flavor

Nutritional Benefits

Custard apple is not just delicious but also nutritionally rich:

  • High in dietary fiber that aids digestion
  • Rich in vitamin C and B-complex vitamins
  • Good source of potassium, magnesium, and copper
  • Contains antioxidants that help fight free radicals
  • Has significant amounts of iron, beneficial for anemia prevention
  • Low in sodium and cholesterol-free
  • Contains compounds that may help regulate blood sugar levels

In Ayurveda, custard apple is considered a "cold" food, making it ideal for consumption during hot weather and to balance "pitta" disorders.

Cultural Significance

In Indian culture, custard apple holds special importance:

  • It's mentioned in ancient Ayurvedic texts for its medicinal properties
  • The fruit is associated with goddess Sita, making it culturally significant
  • In many regions, it's considered auspicious to offer custard apples during certain religious ceremonies
  • Traditional folk remedies use various parts of the custard apple plant
  • In royal courts of ancient India, custard apples were served as a delicacy

Culinary Uses

While mostly enjoyed fresh, custard apple has several culinary applications:

  • Fresh Consumption - Most commonly eaten by scooping out the flesh
  • Sitaphal Rabri - A rich, creamy dessert combining custard apple with milk
  • Custard Apple Ice Cream - A popular flavor in Indian ice cream parlors
  • Sitaphal Kheer - A traditional Indian pudding
  • Milkshakes and Smoothies - The creamy texture makes it perfect for beverages
  • Custard Apple Halwa - A sweet dish made from the pulp
  • Sitaphal Basundi - A thickened milk dessert flavored with custard apple

The seeds, though not edible, are sometimes used in traditional medicine after proper processing.

Growing Season

In India, custard apples typically ripen from late summer through fall (August to November). They thrive in tropical and subtropical regions, particularly in the Deccan Plateau of central and southern India.

Interesting Facts

  • The custard apple tree is deciduous and can grow up to 20 feet tall
  • The flowers have a unique structure that prevents self-pollination
  • Each segment of the fruit develops from an individual flower carpel
  • The seeds contain oil that has been traditionally used as an insect repellent
  • Despite its name, it's not related to apples at all but belongs to the Annonaceae family
  • Research suggests that extracts from custard apple leaves may have anti-diabetic properties
