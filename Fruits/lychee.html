Lychee - The Royal Fruit

The succulent, aromatic fruit with a rich heritage in India

About Lychee

Lychee (Litchi chinensis) is a tropical fruit native to Southern China but has been cultivated in India for centuries. It belongs to the Sapindaceae family, along with rambutan and longan. In India, it is commonly known as "Litchi" or "Lichi."


Lychee was introduced to India during the 18th century and is now primarily grown in Bihar, West Bengal, Jharkhand, Uttarakhand, and parts of Uttar Pradesh. Bihar's Muzaffarpur district is particularly famous for its high-quality lychees and hosts an annual lychee festival.

Popular Indian Lychee Varieties

  • Shahi - The most popular variety in India, known for its large size, sweet taste, and small seed
  • China - Features medium-sized fruits with excellent flavor and aroma
  • Purbi - Early-maturing variety with good quality fruit
  • Bombai - Known for its distinct flavor and moderate sweetness
  • Dehradun - A variety specific to the Uttarakhand region
  • Calcuttia - Primarily grown in West Bengal with unique flavor profile
  • Bedana - Prized for its very small seed and extra-sweet pulp
  • Kasba - Features large fruits with good quality

Nutritional Benefits

Despite its sweet taste, lychee is relatively low in calories and offers numerous health benefits:

  • Rich in vitamin C - one serving can provide over 100% of daily requirements
  • Good source of dietary fiber for digestive health
  • Contains polyphenols with antioxidant properties
  • Provides minerals like potassium, copper, and phosphorus
  • Contains B-complex vitamins including B6, niacin, and folate
  • Has a high water content (about 82%), making it hydrating

Traditional Ayurvedic medicine recognizes lychee for its cooling properties, making it particularly valued during hot summer months.

Cultural Significance

In India, lychee holds special cultural importance:

  • Known as the "Queen of Fruits" in northern India
  • Historically, lychee was a favorite fruit of the Mughal emperors
  • In Bihar, the beginning of lychee season is celebrated with festivities
  • Traditionally offered as premium gifts during its short season
  • Featured prominently in local folklore and stories in lychee-growing regions

Culinary Uses

Lychee is versatile in Indian cuisine and beverages:

  • Fresh Consumption - Most commonly enjoyed fresh as a dessert fruit
  • Lychee Juice and Sharbat - Popular refreshing summer drinks
  • Lychee Kulfi and Ice Cream - Traditional Indian frozen desserts
  • Lychee Rabri - A sweet dish made with lychees and condensed milk
  • Canned and Preserved Lychees - For year-round availability
  • Lychee Chutney - A sweet and tangy condiment
  • Lychee Wine - Produced in some regions of India

Growing Season

In India, lychee has a notably short season, typically from May to June. The exact timing varies by region, with harvest starting earlier in warmer southern areas and later in northern states. This brief availability contributes to its premium status and cultural significance.

Economic Importance

Lychee cultivation is economically significant for many Indian farmers:

  • Bihar alone produces approximately 40% of India's total lychee production
  • India is the second-largest producer of lychee in the world after China
  • The fruit commands premium prices due to its limited season and popularity
  • Provides seasonal employment in growing regions during harvest season
  • Export of fresh and processed lychees contributes to agricultural trade

Interesting Facts

  • The lychee tree can live for hundreds of years and still bear fruit
  • The name "lychee" is derived from the Chinese word "lizhi"
  • A mature lychee tree can produce up to 500 pounds of fruit per season
  • The red rough outer skin is inedible, while the translucent white flesh inside is consumed
  • Muzaffarpur in Bihar is often called the "Lychee Capital of India"
